It is derived from the seeds of&nbsp;<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psoralea_corylifolia" target="_blank">Psoralea Corylifolia</a>, aka Babchi,&nbsp;a&nbsp;plant important in <strong>Indian and Chinese medicine</strong>. The molecule was first isolated in 1973 and several anti-something properties are known about it: it has <strong>anti-inflammatory, antioxidant, anti-tumor, anti-bacterial&nbsp;</strong>and hepatoprotective magical abilities like plenty of other Ayurvedic plant extracts.</p>

<p>What makes&nbsp;Bakuchiol a <strong>special snowflake</strong>&nbsp;is the recent discovery that it <strong>behaves on the skin in a way very similar to well-known <a href="https://incidecoder.com/ingredients/retinol" target="_blank">skincare superstar, retinol</a></strong>. While chemically, it has nothing to do with the <a href="https://incidecoder.com/search?query=retinoids" target="_blank">vitamin-A family, aka retinoids</a>, comparative gene expression profiling (a fancy way of saying that they compared how retinol and bakuchiol modify the way &nbsp;skin cells behave&nbsp;and produce&nbsp;important skin proteins such as collagen) shows that retinol and bakuchiol regulate skin cell behavior in a similar way.&nbsp;</p><p><button class="link-like readmore arrowleft16 fs16">Expand to read more</button></p> <div class="display-none">

<p>To be more specific, both Bakuchiol and retinol <strong>upregulate&nbsp;collagen I, III and IV production</strong> and downregulate&nbsp;MMP, the evil collagen attacking&nbsp;enzyme in our skin. This means more collagen stays in our skin and we all know that more collagen equals <strong>firmer, more wrinkle-free skin</strong>.&nbsp; A 12-week&nbsp;clinical study using a 0.5% Bakuchiol formula with 17 people using it twice a day confirmed a significant improvement in lines and wrinkles, elasticity, firmness and an overall reduction in photo-damage. Also, the test formula was very well tolerated, without any retinol-like side effects.&nbsp;</p>

<p>What&#39;s more, a 2018 <strong>double-blind&nbsp;study</strong> with 44 people compared<strong> 0.5% Bakuchiol with 0.5% retinol</strong> cream and found that &quot;<em><strong>bakuchiol is comparable to retinol</strong> in its ability to improve photoaging&nbsp;and is better tolerated than retinol</em>&quot;. A super promising result after 12 weeks.&nbsp;</p>

<p>If you are not a daredevil-type who doesn&#39;t want to stop using a super well-proven retinol for a newbie like Bakuchiol, we have good news. The two also work&nbsp;very nicely together and <strong>Bakuchiol can actually help to&nbsp;stabilize</strong> the otherwise unstable and hard to formulate<strong> retinol</strong>.</p>

<p>And we are still not done, as Bakuchiol shows not only anti-aging properties but also nice <strong>anti-acne</strong>&nbsp;effects. 1% Bakuchiol seems to be <strong>very effective in itself</strong> (57% reduction in acne after 6 weeks treatment) and <strong>even better when combined with 2% <a href="https://incidecoder.com/ingredients/salicylic-acid" target="_blank">salicylic acid</a></strong>&nbsp;(67% reduction in acne after 6 weeks). We like that Bakuchiol is such a good team player!&nbsp; The molecule works against acne in multiple ways: It downregulates 5&alpha;-reductase (a&nbsp;sebum-controlling enzyme), it is antibacterial (including P. acnes), anti-inflammatory and it inhibits lipid-peroxidation, an evil oxidative process that is recently thought to be a very early trigger in the acne process.&nbsp;</p>

<p>We feel that this description is becoming very long so we will just mention that Bakuchiol also seems to <strong>positively regulate hydration-related genes</strong> such as Aquaporin 3 and also shows some <strong>melanin-inhibiting properties</strong>.&nbsp;</p>

<p><strong>Overall</strong>, we think Bakuchiol is an awesome&nbsp;molecule with <strong>lots of promise both for anti-aging and anti-acne</strong>. But the proof compared to the well-established superstars is far from solid, so in a skincare routine, we would rather add Bakuchiol next to retinol than straight up replace it. Unless you are a gimme-the-newest-shiny-thing-under-the-sun-type.&nbsp;</p>

                <span class="value"><a href="/ingredient-functions/antimicrobial-antibacterial"
                        class="product-long-ingredfunc-link"
                        >antimicrobial/antibacterial</a>, <a href="/ingredient-functions/perfuming"
                        class="product-long-ingredfunc-link"
                        >perfuming</a></span>
            </span></div><div class="ingreddescbox"><p>We have to start by writing how fascinated we are by the amazing lavender fields of Provance and we do love pretty much everything about lavender: its look, its color,&nbsp;its scent.... but, when it comes to skincare, <strong>lavender is a&nbsp;questionable ingredient</strong> that you probably do not want in your skincare products.</p><p>First, let us start with the pros: it has a <strong>lovely scent</strong>, so no wonder that it is popular as a fragrance ingredient&nbsp;in natural products wanting to be free from synthetic fragrances but still wanting to smell nice. The scent of lavender is&nbsp;famous for having <strong>calming and relaxing properties</strong> and some smallish scientific studies do support that. Inhaled volatile compounds seem&nbsp;to have a soothing effect on the central nervous system and studies have shown that lavender aromatherapy can&nbsp;improve patient&#39;s anxiety and experience in hospitals. &nbsp;&nbsp;</p><p><button class="link-like readmore arrowleft16 fs16">Expand to read more</button></p> <div class="display-none"><p>Another pro is that lavender oil has some nice <strong>antimicrobial and antibacterial properties</strong>. It also has some <strong>local&nbsp;pain relieving and muscle relaxing magical powers</strong>. Lavender oil is also often claimed to have anti-inflammatory properties. We have found a study confirming this but it was the essential oil of the leaves and not the much more commonly used flowers and the two differ in their main chemical compounds very much. (The main components of the flower essential oil are linalyl acetate and linalool [around 80% the two together] while it is 1,8-Cineole [around 65%] in the essential oil of the leaves.)</p><p>Now, let us look at the cons: similar to a bunch of other essential oils, the <strong>main components</strong> of lavender oil&nbsp;are <strong>potentially irritating fragrant components</strong>. The two main components are&nbsp;<em>linalyl acetate (about 50%) and linalool (about 35%)</em> and both autoxidise on exposure to the air forming strong contact allergens. To make things even worse, lavender oil seems to be <strong>cytotoxic from concentrations as low as 0.25%</strong> (concentration up to 0.125% were&nbsp;ok).&nbsp;</p><p>There is also an often cited Japanese study that made patch tests with lavender oil for 9 years and found a huge increase in lavender oil sensitivity in 1997 (from 1.1% in 1990 to 8.7% in 1997 and 13.9% in 1998). This was the year when using dried lavender flowers in pillows, wardrobes, and elsewhere became fashionable in Japan, so it seems that <strong>increased exposure to lavender&nbsp;results in increased risk of sensitivity</strong>.</p><p>Overall, it makes us sad to write bad things about such a lovely plant, but when it comes to skincare, you will be better off without lavender.&nbsp;</p></div></div></div><div class="ingred-long showmore-mobile showmore-desktop ">
